Strategic Legal and Technical Architecture for SaaS Dance School Platforms on Google Cloud: A GDPR and UODO Compliance Analysis
Executive Summary
The engineering and deployment of a Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) platform tailored for Polish dance schools, leveraging the computational capabilities of Google Cloud Platform (GCP), Vertex AI, and cross-platform mobile applications, necessitates an intricate alignment of cloud architecture with stringent European and Polish data protection frameworks. The foundational architecture of this system relies on a Parent-Child relational database model, wherein the parent or legal guardian exercises administrative control over the profiles and data of minor students. This structural reality places the platform under the direct regulatory scrutiny of the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), the Polish Act on the Protection of Personal Data, and the highly specific, often restrictive jurisprudence of the Polish Personal Data Protection Office (Urząd Ochrony Danych Osobowych, UODO).

This comprehensive report delivers an exhaustive legal-technical analysis spanning three primary pillars of the proposed system architecture. First, it investigates the integration mechanics of the "Sign in with Apple" authentication protocol, specifically deconstructing the "Hide My Email" private relay service to ensure the platform flawlessly executes the information obligations mandated by Article 13 of the GDPR. Second, the analysis delves into the classification of video materials captured during dance lessons for algorithmic processing via Vertex AI. It critically evaluates whether pose estimation constitutes biometric data processing requiring explicit Article 9 consent, while establishing retention architectures that align with UODO’s severe limitations on the prolonged storage of minors' data. Finally, the report architects a cryptographically secure, immutable audit logging infrastructure utilizing Google Cloud Storage. This WORM (Write-Once-Read-Many) compliant system is engineered specifically to withstand a formal UODO inspection, providing mathematically verifiable proof of parental consent while maintaining compatibility with the data subject's right to erasure.

Part 1: Identity Access Management and Article 13 Transparency Architecture
The implementation of federated identity providers, particularly Apple's native authentication frameworks, introduces complex data routing challenges that directly intersect with the transparency principles of European data protection law. Under Polish jurisdiction, reflecting the parameters of Article 8 of the GDPR, the processing of personal data of a child in relation to information society services is lawful only if the child is at least 16 years old. For dance students below this threshold, the platform must rely on the consent authorized by the holder of parental responsibility. Consequently, the parent serves as the primary authenticated entity within the GCP Identity Platform, legally representing the child's relational database node.   

The Cryptographic and Routing Mechanics of "Hide My Email"
Apple's "Sign in with Apple" protocol is engineered around a zero-trust, privacy-by-design paradigm. During the initial mobile application onboarding, a parent may elect to utilize the "Hide My Email" feature. When activated, the Apple authentication server intercepts the user's authentic Apple ID email address and provisions a unique, randomized, app-specific relay address. Historically, these proxy addresses were issued under the @privaterelay.appleid.com domain, though Apple has systematically transitioned new relay generation to the unified @private.icloud.com namespace.   

The intended functionality of this relay is to facilitate bidirectional communication. Emails dispatched by the SaaS platform's backend to this proxy address are received by Apple's servers, decrypted, and subsequently forwarded to the parent's verified personal inbox without ever exposing the true address to the platform operator. However, this relay network operates under strict cryptographic enforcement. If the GCP backend attempts to transmit a transactional email (such as a privacy policy notification or a consent receipt) from a domain that has not been explicitly authorized, Apple's mail servers will automatically drop the transmission, citing an "unauthorized sender" error.   

To technically satisfy this infrastructure requirement within a Google Cloud environment, a highly specific configuration must be executed within the Apple Developer Portal. The SaaS operator must register all fully qualified domain names (FQDN) and subdomains utilized for outbound communication. Furthermore, Apple mandates absolute alignment of cryptographic email authentication protocols. The registered domains must be fortified with Sender Policy Framework (SPF) DNS TXT records and DomainKeys Identified Mail (DKIM) signatures. Specifically, the envelope sender verification process requires that the DKIM domain—represented by the d= value within the cryptographic signature—matches precisely with the domain utilized in the email's From: header. Only upon successful cryptographic validation will Apple's Private Email Relay forward the critical compliance documentation to the parent.   

Reconciling Native Authentication with Article 13 Obligations
Article 13 of the GDPR imposes a strict temporal requirement on data controllers. It mandates that when personal data are collected directly from a data subject, the controller must provide specific information—including the identity of the controller, the purposes of the processing, the legal basis, and the retention period—at the exact time the data are obtained.   

In traditional web-based registration flows, this is seamlessly accomplished by presenting hyperlinked legal documents adjacent to the account creation button. However, native iOS authentication utilizing the ASAuthorizationController framework triggers a system-level bottom sheet that overtakes the application's user interface. The SaaS application developer exercises zero control over the content or layout of this native Apple overlay, rendering it impossible to inject customized Article 13 privacy notices into the biometric authentication prompt itself.   

To achieve unassailable legal and technical compliance, the platform architecture must intelligently decouple the presentation of the Article 13 notice from the execution of the authentication payload. This decoupling strategy requires a multi-layered interface design. Prior to allowing the parent to trigger the Apple authentication sheet, the mobile application must display a mandatory, pre-authentication onboarding view. This interface must clearly articulate the platform's purpose, identify the data controller, and provide unimpeded access to the complete Privacy Policy and Terms of Service.   

Upon the successful biometric authentication by the parent and the subsequent reception of the JSON Web Token (JWT) from Apple, the application must intercept the user before finalizing the Parent-Child entity creation in the GCP backend database (such as Cloud SQL or Firestore). The system must route the parent to an interstitial confirmation screen. Here, the platform re-presents the core Article 13 information and requires the parent to execute a distinct, affirmative action—such as interacting with an unticked checkbox—to demonstrate their informed consent to the processing terms.

The platform architecture must asynchronously dispatch a highly detailed welcome communication containing a durable, time-stamped copy of the accepted Privacy Policy and Terms of Service to the private.icloud.com address immediately upon the generation of the relational database record. By guaranteeing the delivery of this documentation through strict SPF/DKIM alignment, the SaaS platform definitively satisfies the transparency and communication modalities demanded by Article 12 and Article 13 of the GDPR, ensuring the parent maintains a persistent record of the legal agreement governing their child's data.   

Part 2: Vertex AI, Pose Estimation, and the Biometric Data of Minors
The central technological innovation of the proposed SaaS platform involves the algorithmic ingestion and analysis of video materials depicting children engaged in dance instruction. By utilizing advanced machine learning models, such as those available through Google Vertex AI, the platform intends to execute pose estimation, evaluate kinematic alignment, and provide automated technical feedback. This computational pipeline introduces extraordinary legal exposure, as video footage of natural persons constitutes personal data, and its algorithmic dissection heavily implicates the processing of special categories of data under Article 9 of the GDPR, governed by rigorous enforcement standards set by UODO.   

The Threshold of Biometric Classification
Under Article 4(14) of the GDPR, biometric data is explicitly defined as personal data resulting from specific technical processing relating to the physical, physiological, or behavioral characteristics of a natural person, which allow or confirm the unique identification of that natural person, such as facial images or dactyloscopic data.   

The legal analysis hinges on a highly technical distinction: does the Vertex AI pipeline merely evaluate a human body in a generalized, abstract spatial context (pose estimation), or do the extracted mathematical representations inherently identify the specific child? If the machine learning model maps facial landmarks, analyzes gait signatures that are uniquely attributable to a specific individual, or generates any form of biometric template that can be queried against a database to verify identity, the processing crosses the threshold into biometric data processing.   

The European Data Protection Board (EDPB) Guidelines 3/2019 concerning the processing of personal data through video devices established that the mere optical capture of digital images does not automatically equate to biometric processing; the classification requires the application of specific technical processing aimed at unique identification. However, the regulatory environment is rapidly evolving toward a more restrictive posture. The EDPB's subsequent guidelines on facial recognition and biometric technologies assert that even if the primary commercial objective is not identification, the generation of highly specific physical templates poses severe risks to fundamental rights, often bringing the processing under the purview of Article 9. Given that dance analysis requires the precise, mathematical mapping of a child's bodily proportions, joint mechanics, and behavioral movement patterns, a conservative and legally defensive architecture must classify this output as biometric data.   

UODO Jurisprudence and the Absolute Protection of Minors
The Polish Personal Data Protection Office (UODO) maintains an exceptionally uncompromising stance regarding the processing of minors' biometric data, establishing precedents that profoundly impact the design of the SaaS platform. The foundational enforcement action defining this landscape is the "Gdańsk School" case. In this scenario, a primary school deployed a biometric fingerprint scanning system to verify students' eligibility to receive meals in the cafeteria.   

The President of UODO imposed a substantial administrative fine against the educational institution, establishing three critical pillars of biometric jurisprudence in Poland. First, UODO ruled that the processing was fundamentally disproportionate. Utilizing biometric data for a mundane administrative function violated the core principle of data minimization established in Article 5(1)(c) of the GDPR. Second, UODO systematically invalidated the legal basis of consent. The school argued that parents had provided written, explicit consent for the fingerprinting. However, UODO determined that the inherent power imbalance between the institution and the students rendered the consent invalid, as students who opted out faced unequal treatment and social stigmatization. Third, the ruling codified the principle of absolute necessity, declaring that biometric systems in environments involving children must never be deployed if any less intrusive alternative exists.   

While the SaaS platform operates in a commercial, private-sector context rather than a statutory public education environment, the protective principles established by UODO remain fully applicable. The processing of a child's unique physical characteristics via Vertex AI is inherently viewed as high-risk. To utilize Article 9(2)(a) (explicit consent) as the legal basis for overcoming the general prohibition on processing special category data, the platform must architect a flawless, unbundled consent mechanism.   

The consent interface must strictly adhere to the prohibition on tying or bundling (the coupling prohibition). The parent must possess the absolute freedom to utilize the core functionalities of the SaaS platform—such as class scheduling, tuition payments, and general communication—without being compelled to consent to the AI video analysis. The interface must present a distinct, opt-in mechanism specifically detailing that the child's physical movements and bodily geometry will be extracted and analyzed by artificial intelligence. This request must be communicated in clear, non-technical language, explaining precisely what mathematical data is generated, where the inference occurs, and the immediate eradication protocol for the raw footage.   

Intersection with the European Union Artificial Intelligence Act
The regulatory compliance burden is exponentially magnified by the enactment of the European Union Artificial Intelligence Act (Regulation 2024/1689), which imposes a strict, risk-based classification system on algorithmic processing. The AI Act explicitly prohibits certain applications deemed to present an unacceptable risk to fundamental rights.   

Under Article 5 of the AI Act, biometric categorization systems that process data to deduce or infer sensitive characteristics—such as health status, racial origin, or emotional state—are strictly banned. Furthermore, the legislation imposes a general prohibition on the use of emotion recognition systems within educational and workplace environments, barring narrow exceptions for medical or safety reasons. If the SaaS platform's AI models attempt to quantify a child's "engagement," "frustration," or "emotional expression" during a dance routine, it risks triggering this outright prohibition.   

To remain compliant, the Vertex AI models must be strictly constrained to objective, mechanical pose estimation—calculating the spatial coordinates of joints, rotational velocities, and skeletal alignment. Even when restricted to objective kinematics, AI systems utilized for the educational evaluation or assessment of individuals are highly likely to be classified as high-risk systems under Annex III of the AI Act. This classification necessitates the implementation of a comprehensive risk management system, exhaustive technical documentation, continuous human oversight mechanisms, and stringent data governance protocols to ensure the training and inference data are free from systemic biases.

Architecting the Ephemeral Data Retention Pipeline
The principle of storage limitation, codified in Article 5(1)(e) of the GDPR, mandates that personal data be kept in a form permitting identification for no longer than is strictly necessary for the designated processing purposes. In the context of traditional video surveillance (CCTV), Polish regulations and UODO guidelines establish a strict maximum retention period of three months. However, video data ingested specifically for algorithmic inference operates under a fundamentally different paradigm. The retention of such sensitive material must be constrained exclusively by the technical execution time of the AI pipeline.   

To align with UODO's rigorous expectations regarding data minimization, the GCP architecture must implement an Ephemeral Processing Pipeline. When a video is uploaded via the mobile application, it must be transmitted securely to a highly restricted, heavily monitored Google Cloud Storage bucket. Prior to reaching the Vertex AI inference engine, the architecture should ideally route the video through an edge-level or pre-processing Cloud Function designed to obfuscate or redact the facial features of the children. While true mathematical anonymization of video is highly complex—as gait, height, and clothing remain potential identifiers—the systemic application of facial blurring significantly mitigates the severity of the biometric exposure.   

Once the Vertex AI engine completes its inference, generating the non-identifiable mathematical metadata (the skeletal wireframes, joint angles, and performance scoring metrics), the system must execute an automated eradication protocol. A GCP Pub/Sub trigger or an integrated Cloud Function must immediately and permanently purge the raw .mp4 video file from the storage bucket. The algorithmic metadata, stripped of direct identifiers, can be retained to provide historical progress tracking for the user. If the platform's core business model strictly requires the retention of the raw video—for example, to allow the parent to review the performance alongside the AI-generated visual overlay—the retention period must be aggressively minimized (e.g., restricted to 7 or 14 days), explicitly disclosed during the consent process, and technologically enforced via automated Cloud Storage Object Lifecycle Management policies that execute non-recoverable deletions upon expiration.   

Part 3: Architecting Immutable Audit Logs on Google Cloud
The principle of accountability, enshrined in Article 5(2) of the GDPR, places the unequivocal burden of proof upon the data controller. In the event of a regulatory inspection or a formal data subject complaint, UODO will demand incontrovertible, forensic evidence demonstrating exactly which parent legally consented to the processing of their child's data, the precise timestamp of that authorization, and the exact version of the legal documentation presented at that moment. Furthermore, Article 7(1) of the GDPR explicitly states that where processing is based on consent, the controller shall be able to demonstrate that the data subject has consented to processing of his or her personal data. UODO has consistently held that the inability to definitively prove the parameters of consent renders the processing fundamentally unlawful, exposing the organization to devastating administrative penalties.   

The Forensic Vulnerability of Relational Databases
Standard database architectures, such as Cloud SQL for PostgreSQL or Firestore, are inherently designed for mutability. They support standard Create, Read, Update, and Delete (CRUD) operations. If consent logs are stored exclusively as boolean flags within a relational user table, the platform is forensically vulnerable during a regulatory audit. An investigator from UODO could legitimately assert that a compromised service account, a malicious internal actor, or a negligent database administrator could have retroactively executed an UPDATE query to alter the consent status.   

To survive hostile regulatory scrutiny, the audit log architecture must transcend standard database logging. It must capture the precise parameters of the consent transaction and commit those records to Write-Once-Read-Many (WORM) storage, ensuring absolute cryptographic immutability.   

Implementing Google Cloud Storage Object Retention Locks
To architect a forensically unassailable audit trail within the Google Cloud Platform, the system must utilize Google Cloud Storage (GCS) equipped with specialized retention locks. GCP provides two distinct mechanisms for enforcing immutability: Bucket Lock and Object Retention Lock. While Bucket Lock applies a uniform, inflexible retention policy to all objects within a given bucket, Object Retention Lock allows retention mandates to be defined on a granular, per-object basis.   

For compliance with stringent privacy regulations and to provide absolute proof of GDPR compliance to UODO, Object Retention Lock represents the optimal architectural choice. This feature operates in two distinct modes: Unlocked (Governance Mode) and Locked (Compliance Mode).   

When an object is locked in Compliance Mode, Google Cloud enforces an absolute, irreversible prohibition on deletion or modification. Once the retention date is set and locked, it cannot be reduced or removed under any circumstances. It cannot be bypassed by a project owner, a super-administrator, or even through direct intervention by Google Cloud support engineers. This mathematical guarantee of immutability is precisely what is required to satisfy UODO's evidentiary standards.   

The Immutable Consent Streaming Architecture
To operationalize this compliance mechanism, the GCP backend must process consent events through a highly decoupled, secure streaming pipeline. When a parent interacts with the "Accept" button for the Terms of Service or the explicit biometric opt-in within the mobile application, the client generates a comprehensive consent payload.

This payload is transmitted to an API Gateway, which interfaces with a serverless compute layer (such as Cloud Run or Cloud Functions). The compute layer performs critical validation, verifying the authenticity of the Apple JWT to confirm the cryptographic identity of the parent. Upon validation, the backend publishes the consent event to a Pub/Sub topic, ensuring the logging operation does not block the synchronous user registration flow.

A dedicated subscriber service consumes the event from the Pub/Sub topic, formats the data into a highly structured JSON object, and writes it directly to the designated GCS WORM bucket. Immediately upon the successful write operation, the service issues an API command to apply an Object Retention Lock in Compliance Mode for a duration of five to six years. This specific duration strategically aligns with the general statutory limitation periods for civil claims in Poland, ensuring the legal defense remains intact against potential future litigation.

Reconciling Immutability with the Right to Erasure
The implementation of WORM storage introduces a profound conflict with a core tenant of the GDPR: the data subject's right to erasure, commonly referred to as the "right to be forgotten" under Article 17. If a parent formally requests the deletion of their account and the eradication of their child's data, the SaaS platform is legally obligated to comply. However, objects locked in GCS Compliance Mode cannot be deleted prior to the expiration of their retention period, even to satisfy an Article 17 request.   

To reconcile these conflicting legal mandates, the architecture relies heavily on the implementation of cryptographic severing. As designed in the schema above, the immutable audit logs must absolutely never contain raw PII, such as the parent's plaintext email address, the child's legal name, or the raw biometric data. Instead, the logs utilize pseudonymous identifiers (parentSubjectId and childSubjectId).

When an Article 17 erasure request is received, the platform executes a hard delete of the relational database records in Cloud SQL or Firestore, permanently destroying the mapping between the pseudonymous UUIDs and the actual human identities. Once this primary mapping is destroyed, the UUIDs residing in the locked GCS bucket are "orphaned." The immutable log continues to prove that an anonymous user consented to a specific legal document at a specific time, satisfying the audit requirement. However, because the data can no longer be reasonably linked back to a living, identifiable natural person, the log itself is effectively anonymized. Anonymized data falls entirely outside the scope of the GDPR, thereby satisfying the right to erasure while maintaining the structural integrity of the WORM compliance architecture.
